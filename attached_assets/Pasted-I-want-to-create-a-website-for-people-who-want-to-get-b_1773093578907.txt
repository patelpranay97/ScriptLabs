THE INDIAN VIRALITY DNA
These are the key elements that should be present in every script we aim to go viral:
	1.	Start Midstream
• No “setup” or introductions.
• Drop the viewer directly into a memory, thought, or emotional situation.
• Example: “My dad refused to pay $15 for a kid’s haircut.”
	2.	Use Specific Cultural Anchors
• Details that trigger nostalgia: names, foods, locations, products, language.
• Not just “my mom packed lunch”—say “thepla, shaak, daal bhaat.”
• Example: Bournvita, Tarak Mehta, Fair & Lovely, gaddis, steel plates, mandir friends, Great Clips.
	3.	Introduce Tension Early
• Include a clear internal or external pressure: embarrassment, cultural clash, identity conflict, parental expectations, comparison, or generational divide.
• Don’t over-explain—let the tension simmer through action or dialogue.
	4.	Include a Clear Emotional Shift
• Childhood perspective vs. adult reflection.
• Moment of clarity, understanding, humor, or soft contradiction.
• Example: “I used to hate being seen in Indian clothes. Now I wear them with pride.”
	5.	First Payoff by 20–25 Seconds
• Your first emotional or narrative payoff (climax or punchline) should land here to retain attention.
• If the “moment” happens too late (past 30s), people drop off.
	6.	End with Poetic, Not Preachy, Reflection
• Don’t moralize. Don’t summarize the lesson.
• Use a soft emotional bow—nostalgia, irony, or quiet pride.
• Example: “I’ve never stayed in a 5-star hotel in India. But I’ve slept in a living room full of cousins.”
	7.	Optional but Powerful: Sensory Moments
• Add one sensory visual: the smell of poha, the sound of steel plates, the texture of hot rotis.
• Makes the story feel real and visceral.
